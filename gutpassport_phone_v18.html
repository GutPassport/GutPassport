Chapter 01, The Science

Why it happens

You weren't unlucky.
Your gut just wasn't ready.

Your gut microbiome has spent your entire life adapting to the bacteria in your specific environment. When you land in SE Asia, your gut suddenly meets an entirely different set of organisms which it has zero defence against. This gap is what causes all the trouble, and understanding it now can change how you think about preparation.

Key insight

It is not that SE Asian food is dirty. Your gut simply has zero immunological memory of these organisms. Locals eat the same food and feel nothing because their microbiome has spent decades building resistance against everything that is new to yours. The good news is that you can give your microbiome a head start before you even board the plane.

Two factors nobody warns you about

🌡
Bacteria double every 20 minutes at 33 degrees
Food that looks and smells fine can go from safe to genuinely problematic in two hours at Bangkok temperatures. The "does it look fresh?" test is far less reliable in 34 degrees heat than 14 degrees. Time and temperature are the invisible risk factors.
✈️
Jet lag disrupts your gut before you eat anything
Research from 2016 found that jet lag alone can cause measurable gut microbiome disruption within 48 hours of landing, even before you have eaten a single risky meal. Beneficial bacteria reduce whilst gut permeability increases, which is why so many travellers feel off on day one despite being careful.
The bottom line

Being careful about food is only half the answer. The other half is preparing your gut before you land.

Chapter 02, The Spectrum
03

Not everything is food poisoning

That bloated feeling
on day two? Probably
not food poisoning.

Most travellers come back with one of two stories: "I got food poisoning" or "I was fine." Almost nobody talks about the middle ground, which is where most gut disruption actually happens, and where people suffer without ever understanding what is going on.

Very common
Gut Dysbiosis
  • Bloating, uncomfortable gas and constipation
  • Irregular and unpredictable bowel
  • Sluggish, heavy, genuinely off
  • No fever, no vomiting
  • Can ruin 3 to 5 days of your trip
Common
Travellers' Diarrhoea
  • 3 or more loose stools per day
  • Acute cramping
  • Nausea, possible fever
  • Linked to a meal
  • Rapid onset
  • Resolves within 1 to 5 days
Less common
Severe Infection
  • Fever above 38.5 degrees
  • Blood in stool
  • Severe dehydration
  • Beyond 72 hours
  • Needs medical care
~35%
of travellers get constipation rather than diarrhoea in SE Asia. 3 to 5 days of being constipated in tropical heat, feeling bloated and sluggish while everyone else is at the beach, is not something most travel guides ever prepare you for. An undisrupted gut microbiome is essential for gut motility.

Know the difference

Gut dysbiosis. Your gut is adjusting, not infected.

Gut dysbiosis is microbiome disruption without a specific infection. Your gut bacteria have simply encountered a new environment and have lost their equilibrium, resulting in 3 to 5 days of persistent bloating, constipation and a real sluggishness. Most travellers push through it without realising their gut could have been prepared for this.

Probiotics make a meaningful difference here

Travellers who start a probiotic 7 days before departure are significantly less likely to experience gut dysbiosis at all, because a better-prepared microbiome has far stronger resistance against the incoming bacteria. When dysbiosis does occur in prepared travellers, it tends to be shorter and considerably milder. The microbiome you arrive with matters more than most people ever consider.

Gut dysbiosis
Gradual onset over 1 to 2 days after arrival

Rapid onset within hours
3 or more loose stools per 24 hours

Getting this distinction right is what actually matters. Treating dysbiosis with Imodium, which slows gut motility further, can often make your constipation worse and extend your discomfort by days. For dysbiosis, the answer is generally water, movement and time. For travellers' diarrhoea, it is oral rehydration solution (ORS) the moment you feel the first sign.

The Imodium rule

If you have no fever and no blood, then Imodium is usually appropriate. However, the presence of fever or blood means it is generally advisable to visit a clinic, as these are signs your body is actively trying to expel an infection. Stopping that process with Imodium may make it worse.

Chapter 03, Prevention
04

The playbook

8 rules. Every country.
No exceptions.

Precise, science-backed rules that apply across SE Asia. Thailand, Vietnam, Bali, Cambodia, everywhere.

🧊Rule 01
The Ice Rule
Cylindrical ice with a hole is machine-made from purified water. Crushed or cloudy ice may be tap water, so avoid it in everything.
Freezing does not kill bacteria. It simply pauses them until the ice melts into your drink.
👥Rule 02
The Queue Rule
A queue means high turnover. Food is cooked continuously and nothing sits in the heat. A quiet stall at peak dinner time is a red flag worth taking seriously.
At 33 degrees, bacteria double every 20 minutes.
🥬Rule 03
The Garnish Rule
Remove raw garnishes every time: bean sprouts, fresh herbs, chilli. It is usually rinsed in tap water and is where the risk lives.
The cooked dish beneath is fine. Heat kills bacteria. Raw garnishes get none.
🍴Rule 04
The Cutlery Rule
Always dry wet cutlery with a clean tissue. Washed in tap water and air-dried, any residual moisture on the surface is a risk.
60% of "washed" utensils in SE Asia test positive for coliform bacteria.

Chapter 03, Prevention
05
🚰Rule 05
The Tap Rule
Not safe for drinking, making ice, rinsing fruit or brushing teeth. In Bali, toothbrushing with tap water is a documented cause of gut illness. Bottled water for teeth is non-negotiable.
You do not need to swallow it. Bacteria are absorbed through gum tissue.
🦐Rule 06
The Seafood Rule
Live shellfish from clear, flowing tanks only. Shellfish concentrate whatever bacteria are in the water. A still or murky tank is a genuine risk, so trust your instincts.
Shellfish from contaminated tanks have been linked to Vibrio, Salmonella and norovirus outbreaks across SE Asia.
🍋Rule 07
The Acid Myth
Lime and chilli do not make food safe. Both have antimicrobial properties at high concentrations, but not at seasoning levels. Som tam is not safe because of the lime.
Killing ETEC requires pH below 4.0. A lime-dressed salad sits at pH 5 to 6.
🧼Rule 08 ★ Most important
The Hands Rule
Soap and hot water before eating is essential. Sanitiser can miss certain norovirus strains and Giardia cysts. Your hands are a primary transmission route in SE Asia.
30 to 40% of gut illness in SE Asia is hand-to-mouth, not from contaminated food directly.
If you follow only one rule, make it Rule 08

Handwashing with soap and water is the only intervention with evidence against every transmission route simultaneously. Every other rule addresses a specific risk. This one addresses all of them.

Chapter 04, High-Risk Foods
06

Know before you order

Eat everything.
Just eat it smart.

This is not a list of things to avoid. We are informing you about some key risks so you can make an informed choice in the moment. SE Asian food culture is extraordinary and you should not miss out due to fear.

Highest risk
🥩
Lawar (Bali)
Red lawar contains raw minced pork or blood. A Balinese tradition rather than poor hygiene, but a real risk for travellers. Not always clearly described on menus.
Ask about raw meat first
🍢
Chicken Satay
Campylobacter is SE Asia's dominant pathogen and comes almost entirely from undercooked poultry. Thick skewers look done when they are not.
Check for pink at the centre
Medium-high risk
🥗
Som Tam
Raw papaya rinsed in tap water, served cold. Lime does not kill bacteria. Quiet stalls in the heat are the risk.
Freshly made, busy stalls only
🌱
Pre-Cut Fruit
Bacteria double every 20 minutes at 32 degrees. Two hours sitting means thousands of times more bacteria, invisible to the eye and nose.
Buy whole or freshly cut
Medium risk
🍜
Spring Rolls (Vietnam)
Fresh goi cuon to order is usually fine. The risk is nem chua (raw fermented pork), not always listed on menus at local spots.
Ask about raw pork fillings
🍦
Street Ice Cream
Often made with tap water or unpasteurised dairy. Power cuts cause melt-refreeze cycles that increase bacterial load.
Prefer packaged or coconut shell

Chapter 05, Pre-Trip Protocol
07

Before you fly

7 days before is more
important than day one.

None of this requires giving anything up.

7
Day 7 before departure
Start a probiotic
Look for a high CFU count probiotic which is shelf stable without refrigeration. Starting 7 days before departure provides the optimal time to build up your microbiome and develop colonisation resistance before you land. This is what directly reduces the likelihood of gut dysbiosis and helps speed up recovery if you do unfortunately get travellers' diarrhoea.
5
Day 5 before departure
Add fermented foods daily
Kefir, live yoghurt, kimchi, kombucha or miso. One serving daily for 5 days directly reinforces your microbiome's density and diversity before it faces a new bacterial environment. This matters because a more densely populated microbiome has stronger colonisation resistance.
4
Day 4 before departure
Feed your gut bacteria
Garlic, onions, leeks, slightly unripe bananas, oats, chickpeas and lentils. Prebiotics are the dietary fibre that helps your beneficial bacteria multiply and strengthen before you depart, feeding the bacteria you already have.
3
Day 3 before departure
Hydrate deliberately
Your gut's mucus lining, the first physical barrier between pathogens and your gut wall, is 95% water. Aim for 2 to 2.5 litres daily. Long-haul cabin humidity sits at around 10 to 20%, so arriving well-hydrated means your gut barrier is intact rather than already compromised when you land.
2
Day 2 before departure
Protect your sleep
Research from 2023 found that even two nights of disrupted sleep measurably reduces beneficial Lactobacillus populations and increases gut permeability. You are crossing 7 time zones regardless. Two good nights before departure tops up the reserves before the jet lag begins.
✈
Travel day, carry-on essentials
Pack this before you leave
ORS sachets (at least 6), activated charcoal capsules, your probiotic in carry-on (not hold, temperature affects viability), and a small solid hand soap bar, which is more effective than sanitiser against the pathogens that matter most.
01
First 48 hours in SE Asia
Give your gut a gentle start
Jet lag, dehydration and a disrupted circadian rhythm all increase gut permeability. Your defences are at their lowest point of the entire trip. Stick to hot, freshly cooked food from busy stalls for the first 48 hours. After that, your gut has begun to adapt and your risk profile lowers considerably.
The single most important thing

The earlier you kick off your probiotic routine, the stronger your gut armour will be. Starting a week early is the single biggest game-changer for how your stomach handles arrival in SE Asia.

Chapter 06, Recovery
08

When it hits

You did everything right.
Here's exactly what to do.

Most cases resolve within 48 to 72 hours. How you manage the first 72 determines whether you spend two days off your feet or four, and knowing which condition you are dealing with changes the approach entirely.

🟢 Gut dysbiosis: bloating, constipation, feeling off
  • Water, fruit, movement and prebiotic foods throughout the day
  • Do NOT use Imodium. It slows motility which is already the core problem here
  • Avoid heavy or fried food and lean on plain cooked vegetables and fruit instead
  • Expect this to take 3 to 5 days. It is a real portion of your trip, but preparation shortens it
  • If you started a probiotic before departure, keep taking it. It actively speeds up recovery
🟡 Travellers' diarrhoea, acute onset, 3+ loose stools
  • ORS immediately, not after it gets bad. Take it at the first sign
  • Rest completely and follow the BRAT diet (Banana, Rice, Apple, Toast)
  • Imodium is appropriate only if there is no fever and no blood in the stool
  • If you started a probiotic before departure, your gut was better positioned going into this. Keep taking it throughout recovery as it actively supports your gut flora as it fights back

Your recovery kit, 7-Eleven and pharmacy

💊
ORS Sachets
First priority. Activates gut water absorption in a way plain water simply cannot.
⚫
Ca-R-Bon
Activated charcoal can bind bacterial toxins. Do not take alongside other medications.
🥤
Pocari Sweat
At every 7-Eleven. Better electrolyte profile than water. Good between ORS sachets.
🍌

BRAT diet basics. Bananas firm the gut. Plain rice gives energy without irritation.
🦠

Available at 7-Eleven. Live bacteria to begin repopulating gut flora. A support tool, not a treatment.

Imodium
Only if no fever AND no blood in the stool. Not appropriate for gut dysbiosis.
The Imodium rule, read before you take it

If your illness involves fever above 38 degrees or blood in the stool, then your body is actively trying to expel a bacterial infection. Stopping that process with Imodium keeps the bacteria inside longer and makes things worse. No fever and no blood: Imodium is safe. Either present: clinic instead.

Know when to stop self-managing

When to see
a doctor.

Most cases do not need a clinic. But recognising the signals early, rather than simply pushing through, matters more than most people think.

SymptomLevelAction
Bloating, constipation, off feeling, no feverDysbiosisWater, fruit, movement. 3 to 5 days. No Imodium.
Loose stools, mild nausea, no feverMild TDORS, rest, BRAT diet. Monitor 24 hours.
6 or more stools per day, vomitingModerateORS aggressively. Full rest. Doctor if not improving in 24 hours.
Fever above 38.5 degrees with diarrhoeaSee doctorNo Imodium. Go to a clinic: bacterial infection pattern requiring evaluation.
Blood or mucus in stoolSee doctorGo immediately. Possible dysentery: needs medical treatment, not self-care.
No improvement after 72 hoursSee doctorCould be Giardia (parasitic) which requires a prescription antiparasitic to clear.
Confusion, no urination for 8+ hoursEmergencyHospital immediately. Severe dehydration is genuinely dangerous.

The first hour action plan

Most people make it worse by panicking or ignoring the early signs. The first hour matters more than the next 24.

1
Stop eating, water only
Your gut needs to stop processing new food so it can focus on what is already inside it. Do not try to eat through it.
2
Start ORS immediately, not after it gets bad
Dissolve a sachet the moment you feel the first signs. The window for preventing severe dehydration is much earlier than most people think.
3
Check your temperature and note your symptoms
Above 38.5 degrees means clinic, not self-treating. Blood or mucus means go immediately regardless of temperature.
4
Tell someone where you are
If you are travelling solo, message a friend or hostel reception. Serious dehydration can cause confusion quickly so you want someone to be aware.
5
Rest, keep drinking ORS, reassess at 24 hours
Genuinely improving: continue self-managing. No meaningful improvement or worsening: clinic time. Do not push that decision to 48 hours.

⭐ Bonus

Extra for you

The 5-minute
stall safety check.

Run through this before ordering from any stall. You do not need every green signal, but three or more red flags at the same stall and the queue a few doors down is worth a look.

Good signs
Queue of locals at peak times
Food cooked fresh over high heat
Raw and cooked ingredients kept separate
Food visibly steaming when served
Covered storage for uncooked items
Clean and dry work surfaces and utensils
Walk away if you see
Pre-cooked food sitting uncovered in heat
Raw and cooked handled with same utensils
Completely empty stall during busy hours
Food that smells sour, fermented or off
Flies settled on food that is not covered
Seafood that smells even slightly off
Remember, the local standard is generally high

SE Asian street food culture takes hygiene seriously. The vast majority of street food is safe when freshly cooked over high heat and served immediately. These signals are about spotting the exceptions, not about avoiding the food.

Always carry these in SE Asia

💊 ORS sachets (pack at least 6)
⚫ Activated charcoal capsules
🧼 Small solid hand soap bar
🧻 Pocket tissue pack for cutlery
🌡 Small digital thermometer
💊 Probiotic, carry-on always

The thermometer is the thing most people forget. If you cannot tell whether you have a fever, then you cannot make the right call about whether to see a doctor, and that decision matters more than anything else in this guide.
